Official standards & guidelines

ibm.com/standards/web/design/breadcrumbs/


What

A navigation trail (AKA: breadcrumb trail) on a page indicates the page's position in the site hierarchy. A user can navigate all the way up in the site hierarchy, one level at a time by starting from the last breadcrumb in the breadcrumb trail. Breadcrumbs are not to show where the user has been. That's what their back button and browser history is for. A user always knows how to get back where they came from - their back button. But they never know where they land in the hierarchy of site when coming in from a search or shared URL.


Why

The breadcrumb trail helps the user understand where they are in the taxonomy or hierarchy of a site. And with the IBM web presence at 10+ million pages strong... is there any question why we need it?


What's new

The <nav> wrapper and the addition of the schema.org micro data tagging/wrappers are new from v17 HTML. These are optional but highly recommended. When you mark up breadcrumb information in the body of a web page, Google can use it to understand and present the information on your pages in their search results. More information can be found on the Google Developer Structured Data Markup page and schema.org breadcrumb list sites.


Rules & guidelines

  • The breadcrumb trail is optional, but effective when used.
  • Do not include "IBM" as the top level breadcrumb item.
  • Do not include the current page as the lowest level breadcrumb item.
  • The breadcrumb should fill the hierarchy void between the current page and the ibm.com home page.
  • The breadcrumb will work anywhere in the content space. The technical requirement of it only working inside ibm-leadspace-head no longer exists.
